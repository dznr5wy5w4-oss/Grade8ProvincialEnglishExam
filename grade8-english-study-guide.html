What's on the test

The assessment has four parts delivered online. You'll have 45 minutes for each part, plus an extra 15 minutes if needed.

  • Part 1 (Reading): read passages, answer multiple-choice questions
  • Part 2 (Writing): plan and write one piece (story or essay)
  • Part 3 (Reading): more passages and questions
  • Part 4 (Writing): the other writing task type

Across the reading sections, you'll see four genres: information texts, narratives, poetry/song, and visual texts. Each makes up roughly 20–30% of the questions.

How questions are built

Reading questions fall into three thinking levels:

  • Literal (~30–40%) — the answer is stated directly. Find the words.
  • Non-literal (~30–40%) — the answer is implied. You'll need to infer, decode figurative language, or interpret tone.
  • Analysis (~30–40%) — the answer requires you to think across the whole text, evaluate, or synthesize.

Writing is scored on four traits: Ideas, Organization, Language Use, and Conventions. Each is scored 1–4. Aim for 3 (at the expectation) or 4 (above).

The four-trait rubric

Each piece of writing is scored on these four traits, each rated 1–4. Aim for level 3 or 4 on each.

LvlIdeasOrganizationLanguage UseConventions
4 Distinct main idea developed through vivid and relevant details. Skillfully organized; skillful use of varied transitions. Vivid and skillful — strong voice, precise word choice, varied sentences. Consistently correct; conventions enhance communication.
3 Clear main idea developed with relevant details. Organized; effective use of varied transitions. Clear and fluid — good voice, solid word choice. Generally correct; effective communication.
2 Main idea evident, somewhat developed; some details may be irrelevant. Somewhat organized; vague or mechanical transitions. Vague or mechanical writing. Errors are noticeable but communication is coherent.
1 Main idea missing or not developed. Lacking organization; little or no transitions. Confusing writing or little evidence of language use. Errors interfere with communication.

Self-check before you submit

My main idea is clear in the first paragraph.
Each paragraph has one job (one main idea).
I used varied transitions (not just "first, next, then").
I have a mix of short and long sentences.
I used strong, specific verbs.
Capitals are correct: sentence beginnings, names, "I".
I checked homonyms: their/there/they're, your/you're, its/it's.
My ending wraps things up — it doesn't just stop.

Test day tips

Strategies that actually work, broken down by section.

Reading sections

  • Pace yourself: about 10–11 minutes per passage if there are four passages in 45 minutes.
  • Read the question first when you re-read. It tells you what to look for.
  • Eliminate two clearly wrong answers first. Then choose the better of the remaining two.
  • If a question stumps you for 90 seconds, flag it and come back. Don't burn time.
  • Trust the text. The right answer is supported by the passage, not by what you already think.
  • Save 5 minutes at the end to check that every question has an answer.

Writing sections

  • Spend 5–8 minutes planning. Don't skip this. Pick a prompt, jot down 3–5 bullet points, then start writing.
  • Spend 25–30 minutes writing. Don't perfect the opening — get words down, then refine.
  • Spend 5–10 minutes revising. Use the C.U.P.S. checklist.
  • Aim for at least three or four paragraphs. Show your reader you can structure ideas.
  • Show, don't tell. "Her hands clenched into fists" beats "She was scared."
  • Use varied transitions. "Furthermore," "in contrast," "as a result" are stronger than "and then."

The C.U.P.S. revision checklist

  • C — Capitalization. Sentence beginnings, proper nouns, "I".
  • U — Usage. Right words? Verb tenses consistent? Subject-verb agreement?
  • P — Punctuation. Periods, commas, apostrophes, quotation marks.
  • S — Spelling. Especially homonyms: their/there/they're, your/you're, its/it's.

Mindset

  • Some questions will feel hard. That's by design — the assessment is meant to find your edge.
  • A challenging question isn't a sign you're failing; it's a sign you're working at your level.
  • Keep going. Try every question. Don't leave anything blank.
  • Sleep well the night before. Eat breakfast. Bring a positive attitude.
  • You've prepared. You know how to do this.
